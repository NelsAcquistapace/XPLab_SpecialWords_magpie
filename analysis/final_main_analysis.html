Statistical analysis of the main study

Our study replicates Experiment 1A from the paper “What makes a word special? Words as unmotivated cues” (Edmiston & Lupyan, 2015). In this file, the statistical analysis of the main part of the study that followed the pilot study is documented. The complete analysis follows the guidelines of our study preregistration. In the experiment, participants hear an auditory sound cue that is either a word label describing a (basic-level) category (e.g. dog) or an environmental sound (e.g. the bark of a dog). After hearing the auditory cue, a picture of object or animal that belong to a certain category (e.g. a picture of a guitar) was presented. The participants had to indicate via button press, whether the sound cue and the picture belong to the same category.

Hypothesis testing

Now we are ready to start testing our hypotheses! Our two main hypotheses are based on the original paper:
1. Environmental sound cues are not as effective as category labels in activating a concept. That is, reaction times for trials with environmental cues are higher than for trials with verbal labels.
2. Sound cues activate specific category exemplars instead of general categories. That is, reaction times for congruent trials will be lower than for incongruent trials.

In the following analysis we will use linear mixed effects regression models that include our factors of interest as fixed effects (cue_type and sound_type) but also account for random effects, based on recommendations from the paper “Random effects structure for confirmatory hypothesis testing: Keep it maximal” (Barr et. al., 2013). This means that we want to keep the maximal complex model structure, without including unnecessary variables. The random effects account for random intercept and random slope effects of within-subject factors, as well as random intercept effects for unique trial types. A a more detailed explanation of the random effect structure follows below.

# This function is used to compare the complex and the simpler model explained below. The methods used in the analysis we are using are based on and as close as possible to the methods used in the statistical analysis of the original paper.

model_comparison <- function(complex, simple, factor) {
  # First we compute the parameter estimation for the factor of interest
  coeff_complex_model <- summary(complex)$coefficients
  parameter <- coeff_complex_model[factor, 'Estimate']
  std_error <- coeff_complex_model[factor, 'Std. Error']
  
  # Compare the more complex model with the simple model. We compute the chi-square and the p-value that   we use for statistical inference.
  compare_with_simple <- anova(complex, simple)
  chi_sqr <- compare_with_simple['complex', 'Chisq']
  p_value <- compare_with_simple['complex', 'Pr(>Chisq)']
  report_comparison <- sprintf("Estimated parameter = %.2f ,(Std. Error: %.2f), Chi Sqr(1) = %.2f, p = %.4f", parameter, std_error, chi_sqr, p_value)
  
  list(report_comparison, compare_with_simple, parameter, std_error, p_value)
}

Testing the first hypothesis

The first model states that the type of the cue (verbal label or environmental sound cue) correlates with the reaction time of the participants that verify the category match. We hypothesize that if the cue is a verbal label, it leads to faster reaction times than the environmental sound cues. This would mean that verbal labels are more effective than environmental sounds that are produced by instances of the category. We will only look at congruent environmental sound cues as incongruent sound trials could cause confusion of the participants and thus make the results of the comparison between environmental sound cues and verbal labels less reliable. The more complex model is compared to the simpler model that does not include the factor cue_type. Remember that the cue_type verbal label is coded as -0.5 and the environmental cue_type is coded as 0.5. The coding of the factors let us make assumptions about the direction of the effect of the cue_type on the verification speed (reaction time). If the estimated parameter (computed from the linear mixed effects regression model) for the factor cue_type is positive, we can infer that trials with environmental sound cues lead to higher reaction times than trials with verbal label cues. Recall that the reaction time values RT are log-transformed.
Note on the random effects: The size and strength of the effect of the factor cue type on the participants verification speed might vary from subject to subject, thus we added a random slope effect as well as a random intercept effect for within-subjects effect deviations to the more complex model. The random slope effect that captures the deviation of the factor cue_type within participants is not included in the simpler model because we don’t assume here that the cue_type has any effect on the reaction time. Both, the simpler and the more complex model allow a random intercept effect by items because our experimental is composed of unique trials that might induce derivation in reaction times.


For hypothesis 1 we fit the model:
RT ~ cue_type_nr + (1+cue_type_nr|submission_id) + (1|trialID)


We compare it with the basic model:
RT ~ 1 + (1|submission_id) + (1|trialID)


Now we investigate if the more complex model that includes the cue_type as a fixed effect fits better than the more simple model, that does not account for an fixed effect of the cue_type (verbal label vs. environmental cue) on the reaction time.

The parameter for cue_type is slightly positive (CI [-0.003, 0.049]) which means that - if the complex model was right that there is an effect of cue_type - the reaction time is higher for environmental sound cues than for verbal category labels.

Testing the second hypothesis

The second model states that the factor sound_type has an effect on the verification speed of the participants. We hypothesize that if the environmental cue is congruent to the instance of the category subgroup depicted (e.g. hearing a high-pitched dog bark followed by the picture of a small dog), leads to faster verification speed (lower RT) than the incongurent environmental sound cues (e.g. hearing a low-pitched dog bark followed by the picture of a small dog). Based on the original paper we assume that sound cues activate specific category exemplars instead of general categories. The complex model including the sound_type as factor of interest is compared to the simpler model that does not include the factor sound_type. The sound_type congruent is coded as 0.5 and the incongruent sound_type is coded as -0.5. According to the coding of the factor level congruent and incongruent we can state that congruent sound trials lead to lower reaction times, if the estimated parameter (computed from our more complex linear mixed effects regression model) for the factor sound_type is negative. Still this can only be concluded if the calculated p-value is below the significance threshold, which indicates that the model including the factor sound_type actually describes the underlying data appropriately.


Note on random effects: The structure of the linear mixed effects regression model is equivalent to hypothesis 1 models. See above for explanation of random effects structure.


For hypothesis 2 we fit the model:
RT ~ sound_type + (1+sound_type | participantID) + (1 | trialID)


We compare it with the simpler model that does not include the factor sound_type
RT ~ 1 + (1| participantID) + (1 | trialID)


Now we investigate if the more complex model that includes the sound_type as a fixed effect is more likely to fit the observed data better than the more simple model. The simpler model is an intercept-only model and does not include sound_type (congruent vs incongruent) as fixed effect on the reaction time.

- the parameter estimation for the factor sound_type is: B = -0.04, (Std. Error: 0.02) 95% CI [-0.09, -0.00]
- the calculated p-value is: 0.14

Interpretation: The calculated p-value is not below the significance threshold of 0.05. Therefore, we can conclude that the model that includes an effect of the sound type (congruent or incongruent) is not representing the underlying data better than the simpler model that does not include the factor. Contrary to our suppositions, the congruency of sound to the depicted instance of the category subgroup does not seem to have a significant effect on the verification speed of the participants. This contradicts the results of the study by Edminston and Lupyan (2015) who claim that sound activates specific category exemplars, which would lead to faster verification of the category in trials with congruent environmental sound cues. The estimated parameter for sound_type is negative ([-0.086, -0.002]), which means that the reaction time for congruent sound trials is lower than for incongruent sound trials. This result would match our hypothesis 2, but the evidence that there is a non-zero correlation between the independent variable sound_type and the dependent variable RT is insufficient.

Note on the power of our results:
Since we had to exclude participants from our data, the sample size is small and therefore the power of the results is insufficient to claim that there is no effect of the cue type and the sound type on the reaction time. A larger number of participants could lead to a different p-value and reveal an effect of one or both factors on the dependent variable. Referring to the power analysis that we computed before the analysis was done, we would need more than 50 participants to achieve a power of 95%. For comparison: the original study had a number of 43 participants (power of 0.88), whereas we only had 32 valid participants, thus there is only 78% chance that the results of our hypotheses test is meaningful.

Exploratory analysis

In the exploratory analysis we would like to investigate the more complex models above by performing a bayesian regression approach, using the brms package by Paul Buerkner (2016). It enables us to plot the posterior output of our model and lets us explore the model more visually and statistically. Furthermore, we will compare the estimated parameter for our two factors with the function compare_groups() using the faintr package.

# Function to plot the posteriors
plot_posterior <- function(model, data, plot_title) {
  pp_data <- posterior_predict(model, nsamples = 50) 
color_scheme_set("gray" )
plot_dense <- ppc_dens_overlay(y = data, yrep = pp_data) +
  labs(x = "Reaction Time (log transformed)", title = plot_title) +
  theme(title = element_text(size=10))
  plot_dense
}

Exploratory hypothesis - Categories

When plotting the distribution of the reaction times in congruent sound cue trials dependent on categories we can see that there is a slight variation of performance. We assume that in some categories we find the sound more appropriate or more easy to recognize (environmental sound in conjunction with picture of a category) than in others. Here we will only examine the congruent sound trials. If it is true that some categories are more easy to recognize, then it is important to mention that the reduced efficiency of environmental sounds in comparison to verbal labels is to some extent dependent on the category. Some sounds could just be harder to identify, which does not necessarily mean that incongruent environmental sounds trials are significantly more difficult to verify as same category.

We use the Bayesian approach to get a more information about the effect of categories on the verification speed in congruent environmental sound trials. We would like to know if there is a systematic effect of the selected categories on the reaction time that yields for all subjects. Since the plot suggests this effect we would explicitly like to know if animal sounds lead to faster reaction times than sounds belonging to instruments or utility objects.


Since subjects might be variously familiar with certain categories the model will include a random slope and random intercept effect. The random intercept (by-item) effect is included as well, because we still have unique trial type (like in the models above).


Hence our model looks like this:
RT ~ category_sum + (1+ category_sum | participantID) + (1|trialID)

category_mod <- brm(RT_log ~ sum_categories + (1+sum_categories|submission_id) + (1|trialID), congruent_trials, control = list(adapt_delta = 0.99), seed=2001)

To investigate how likely it is that trials with animal categories lead to a lower reaction time than trials with instrument or utility object categories and how likely it is that trials with instrument categories lead to lower/higher reaction time than trials with utility object categories, we use compare_groups().


- In 88% of the cases the sound of an animal is faster to recognize then the sound of an instrument.
- In 95% of the cases the sound of an animal is faster to recognize then the sound of an utility object such as a phone or a motor cycle.
- In 73% of the cases the sound of an instrument is faster to recognize then the sound of an utility object such as a phone or a motor cycle.


- The estimated parameter for the intercept of the model (animal categories) is 6.48 with a confidence interval [6.39, 6.56]. The intercept parameter is the expected average (log transformed) reaction time if the category is an animal. The parameter estimation for instrument categories is 0.04 with CI [-0.02, 0.1], for utility object categories 0.06 with CI [-0.01, 0.13] which means that the reaction time rises slightly, if the category is not an animal but an utility object or an instrument. The confidence interval for the latter two parameters shows that the estimated parameter values move around zero. Thus, the effect of the two categories on the verification speed is not constantly positive. It can be concluded that there is no significant correlation of the different categories on the reaction time on the participants. If we would add more categories to our experimental design this could of course change the results of this hypothesis test, since we do a between category caparison.


- In order to be critical with our model, we observe its posterior distribution and compare it to the underlying data. The plot shows that the model roughly fits but in some points deviates from the data. Because of the insufficient goodness of fit we cannot make statistically reliable conclusions about the effect of different categories on the verification speed.
